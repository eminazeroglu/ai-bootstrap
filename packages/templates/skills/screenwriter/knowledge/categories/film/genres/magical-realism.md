# Magical Realism — Genre

> Films where magic exists in the everyday world, treated as ordinary. Distinguished from fantasy (which has separate magical worlds).

## Defining elements

- Magic / impossible elements present
- Treated as normal by characters
- World otherwise realistic
- Cultural / spiritual roots often
- Subtle vs. spectacular

## Distinguished from related genres

- **Magical realism** = magic in real world, treated as normal
- **Fantasy** = magical world with rules
- **Surreal** = dream-logic, often illogical
- **Sci-fi** = speculative, technology-based

In magical realism, a ghost is just a ghost — the family knows him, talks to him, has dinner with him. Not a horror element.

## Structural conventions

### Magic accepted
- No explanation
- Characters not surprised
- Audience adapts
- Magic just is

### Realistic anchor
- Setting recognizable
- Most events realistic
- Magic punctuates rather than dominates

### Cultural / spiritual context
- Often rooted in folk belief
- Indigenous magic traditions
- Religious / mystical
- Latin American literature roots

### Subtle integration
- Not flashy
- Often subtle visual
- Audience accepts gradually

## Voice / Style

### Visual
- Realistic + heightened moments
- Color saturation often
- Beautiful cinematography
- Light used magically

### Music
- Atmospheric
- Folk instruments often
- Score builds wonder

### Dialogue
- Naturalistic
- Magic referenced casually
- Poetic moments
- Cultural specificity

## Magical realism mechanics

### The threshold
Audience enters magical realism when first impossible thing happens. Reaction:
- Characters: unsurprised
- Audience: re-orients

After threshold, more magic accepted easily.

### Symbolism
- Magic = metaphor often
- Grief becomes ghost
- Love becomes ability
- Memory becomes physical

### Cultural rooting
- Magic comes from culture
- Folk tradition
- Indigenous belief
- Religious framework

### Restraint
- Less is more
- Single magical element can carry film
- Don't over-explain

## Sample magical realism scene

```
INT. GRANDMOTHER'S HOUSE - DAY

Three generations sit around a long table. The food is from a wedding
that happened forty years ago. Everyone knows this. Nobody mentions it.

The GRANDMOTHER (80) serves the soup. She doesn't have to ladle it
— it pours itself when she holds the bowl up.

GRANDMOTHER
Eat. The soup remembers what we forgot.

ELDAR (12) takes a sip. Closes his eyes.

ELDAR
I can taste it.

GRANDMOTHER
What can you taste?

ELDAR
The wedding.

GRANDMOTHER
Which one?

ELDAR
The one where you danced with the man who became my grandfather.

GRANDMOTHER
And what did the dance feel like?

ELDAR
Like rain that wasn't sad.

The grandmother smiles. She continues serving. The soup keeps pouring
itself.

ELDAR'S MOTHER (40)
Mother, leave the boy alone.

GRANDMOTHER
He understands. The understanding skips a generation.

She pours soup for ELDAR'S MOTHER. The mother accepts. Tastes.

MOTHER
... I taste it too.

GRANDMOTHER
Good. The soup is generous today.

She continues to the next bowl.
```

This works through:
- Magic accepted (self-pouring soup)
- Cultural context (memory, food, generation)
- Symbolic meaning (soup = memory of love)
- Naturalistic dialogue
- Subtle wonder
- Multi-generational

## Common mistakes

- Treating magic as fantasy (rules-heavy)
- Treating magic as horror (fear-inducing)
- Explaining the magic
- Magic disconnected from theme
- Generic Western fairy tale
- Magic as plot device only
- Inconsistent (sometimes accepted, sometimes shocking)

## When to write magical realism

- Cultural story
- Family / generational
- Spiritual / philosophical
- Festival-friendly
- Star vehicle (often)
- Adaptation of novel

## Production realities

- Mostly realistic production
- Magic moments may need VFX
- Often achievable on indie budget
- Practical effects preferred
- Period accuracy if applicable

## Combination

- Magical realism + Drama (most common)
- Magical realism + Family (Big Fish)
- Magical realism + Romance (Amélie)
- Magical realism + Coming-of-age (Pan's Labyrinth)
- Magical realism + Folk horror (Pan's Labyrinth — hybrid)
